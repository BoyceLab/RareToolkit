# Why this guide exists

Rare disease communities are often the first place where important longitudinal data comes together. Families track symptoms, specialists, testing, treatments, and outcomes long before those data are harmonized across institutions.

A well-designed registry can help a community:

- describe the condition more clearly,
- support diagnosis and research,
- improve trial readiness,
- contribute data to public knowledge resources,
- and reduce repeated data entry for families.

## Common problems this guide tries to solve

### Free-text only data
Open text is important, but it is hard to compare across participants and sites.

### Questionnaires that are too clinical
Highly technical forms often reduce completion and increase drop-off.

### No data dictionary
Without a shared definition for each field, data quality drops quickly.

### No mapping to standards
Teams may collect valuable information but still struggle to share or reuse it.

### No update workflow
Rare disease data change over time. Registries need a way to capture updates, not just intake.

## Design goal

Use a **two-layer model**:

- **Layer 1:** patient-friendly questions and answer choices.
- **Layer 2:** internal mapping to HPO, disease identifiers, and GA4GH-compatible structures.

That keeps the participant experience approachable while preserving downstream usefulness.
