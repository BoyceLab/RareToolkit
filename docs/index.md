# Rare Disease Patient Advocacy Group's Guide to Data Collection with GA4GH

Patient advocacy groups often collect deeply valuable information: diagnosis history, symptoms, care journeys, family history, treatments, outcomes, and lived experience. This site is a practical guide to collecting that information in a way that is:

- understandable to patients and families,
- useful to clinicians and researchers,
- easier to share responsibly, and
- more reusable over time.

The guide is written for advocacy groups that want to improve registries, natural history studies, research readiness, or patient-contributed data programs.

## What this guide helps you do

1. Understand what **GA4GH** is and why its standards matter.
2. Use the **Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO)** to describe symptoms in a more consistent way.
3. Design questionnaires that are usable for families but still map to structured data.
4. Build toward **Phenopackets**, a GA4GH standard for exchanging phenotype and disease information.
5. Put governance, consent, and trust at the center of data collection.

## A practical principle

Start with plain-language questions for patients.
Then map those answers to standards behind the scenes.

That approach usually works better than exposing technical codes directly in the questionnaire.

## Recommended starting point for small teams

If your group is early-stage, begin with:

- one core intake questionnaire,
- one annual update questionnaire,
- one diagnosis-specific module,
- HPO-backed phenotype mapping,
- a simple data dictionary, and
- a plan for consent and report upload.

## What standards appear in this guide

- **GA4GH**: a global standards body for genomics and health data sharing.
- **HPO**: a standard vocabulary for phenotypic abnormalities.
- **Phenopackets**: a GA4GH format for exchanging structured phenotype and disease data.

## Suggested audience

This guide is for:

- patient advocacy group staff,
- registry leads,
- research program managers,
- genetic counselors and clinicians partnering with advocacy groups,
- data stewards, and
- vendors building registry platforms.
