# Questionnaire design with HPO

## Core design rule

Participants answer **questions**, not ontologies.
Your team manages the ontology mapping.

## Recommended structure

### 1. Start broad
Begin with body-system screening questions such as:

- growth
- neurologic/developmental
- vision
- hearing
- craniofacial
- cardiac
- respiratory
- gastrointestinal
- endocrine
- musculoskeletal
- skin/hair/nails
- immune/hematologic
- renal/genitourinary
- psychiatric/behavioral

### 2. Add targeted follow-up only when relevant
Branching reduces burden and improves completion.

### 3. Capture timing
Phenotype alone is not enough. Timing matters.

Recommended timing fields:

- age symptoms first appeared,
- age at diagnosis or recognition,
- current status,
- whether the issue is ongoing, resolved, intermittent, or unknown.

### 4. Capture source and certainty
Consider fields such as:

- self-reported,
- caregiver-reported,
- clinician-diagnosed,
- from genetic report,
- from medical record,
- unsure.

### 5. Keep free text, but use it strategically
Use free text for:

- “tell us anything important we missed,”
- rare manifestations not covered yet,
- local language descriptions,
- care priorities and lived-experience context.

## Suggested answer pattern

For each phenotype area:

1. Has this ever been present?
2. Is it current?
3. When did it begin?
4. Was it diagnosed by a healthcare provider?
5. Do you have records or test results related to it?

## Things to avoid

- long jargon-heavy symptom lists on a single page,
- mixing diagnosis, symptom, and treatment in one item,
- asking participants to select ontology codes,
- requiring exact dates when a range is acceptable,
- hiding “unsure” when uncertainty is expected.
