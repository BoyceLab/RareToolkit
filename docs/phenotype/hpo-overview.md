# HPO overview

## What is HPO?

The **Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO)** is a standardized vocabulary for describing phenotypic abnormalities seen in human disease.

Instead of one family saying “small head,” another saying “tiny head,” and a clinic note saying “microcephaly,” a registry can map those descriptions to a shared concept.

## Why HPO helps patient registries

HPO makes it easier to:

- compare participants,
- search by phenotype,
- support variant interpretation,
- prepare data for rare disease research,
- and connect your registry to broader data-sharing workflows.

## Best practice for advocacy groups

Do **not** ask participants to browse HPO codes directly unless your audience is highly experienced.

A better pattern is:

- ask in plain language,
- provide examples,
- let people answer "yes / no / unsure" or pick from guided lists,
- then map responses to HPO internally.

## Good examples of HPO-backed survey design

A strong item usually contains:

- a plain-language label,
- examples,
- a mapped HPO term,
- age of onset,
- age at diagnosis or recognition,
- optional severity or frequency.

## Example

| Patient-facing question | Internal mapping |
|---|---|
| Have you had hearing loss? | HPO: Hearing impairment (HP:0000365) |
| If yes, when did it first appear? | Onset field |
| Has a healthcare provider diagnosed this? | Provenance / confirmation |

## When to use broad versus specific terms

Use broad body-system questions for intake, then open targeted follow-up modules for people who answer yes.

For example:

- intake: “Have you had issues with your heart?”
- follow-up: congenital heart defect, arrhythmia, cardiomyopathy, hypertension, surgery history, onset, provider confirmation.
